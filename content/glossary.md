# Glossary

> This glossary contains specialized terms from Indian languages, linguistics, and cultural contexts used throughout the book. Terms include Sanskrit, Hindi, Bengali, and Tamil vocabulary, Indian proper nouns, and academic terminology that may be unfamiliar to readers without a background in South Asian studies.

## A

**aananda** — Bliss or joy (Sanskrit). One of the three aspects of Brahman in Vedantic philosophy: sat-chit-ananda (being-consciousness-bliss). The term appears in the Taittiriiya Upanishad passage that Nehru quotes about freedom. [term]

**aanandaad** — From bliss (Sanskrit ablative case). Part of the Upanishadic verse "aanandaad dhy eva khalv imaani bhuutaani jaayante" (from bliss indeed these beings are born). [term]

**aanandam** — Bliss (Sanskrit accusative case). Object form of aananda used in Upanishadic texts. [term]

**aanandena** — By bliss (Sanskrit instrumental case). Grammatical form indicating agency or instrument in Sanskrit philosophical texts. [term]

**aagama** — Sacred scripture or authoritative text (Sanskrit). Refers to a class of religious texts in Hinduism distinct from the Vedas. [term]

**aaryaavarta** — The land of the noble ones (Sanskrit). Traditional name for the Indian subcontinent, particularly northern India where Vedic culture flourished. [place]

**aatmaa** — Self, soul (Sanskrit). The eternal essence of being in Hindu philosophy, often contrasted with the empirical ego. [term]

**abhisamvisanti** — They enter (Sanskrit). Third person plural verb form from the Taittiriiya Upanishad passage about beings returning to bliss. [term]

**acalaa** — Unmoving, immutable (Sanskrit). Term used in philosophical contexts to describe permanent reality. [term]

**adaptationist** — In linguistics, relating to theories that view language change as adaptation to communicative needs. [linguistics]

**adhunikata** — Modernity (Bengali). The state or quality of being modern, often used in discussions of colonial and postcolonial cultural transformation. [term]

**Ambedkar** — B.R. Ambedkar (1891-1956). Indian jurist, economist, social reformer, and chief architect of the Indian Constitution. Leader of the Dalit movement and convert to Buddhism. [scholar]

**Annamalai** — Central Institute of Indian Languages, formerly in Annamalai University. Important center for Indian linguistics research. [place]

**archinorm** — A theoretical linguistic construct representing the underlying norm from which variants derive. [linguistics]

**Ashtadhyayi** — Pāṇini's Sanskrit grammar treatise, consisting of eight chapters. Foundation of linguistic analysis. [term]

**Aurobindo** — Sri Aurobindo Ghose (1872-1950). Indian philosopher, yogi, and poet who developed integral yoga philosophy and was a key figure in the independence movement. [scholar]

**autophoric** — In linguistics, referring to expressions that derive their meaning from the context of utterance rather than from external reference. [linguistics]

## B

**Bangalore** — Major city in Karnataka, center of India's information technology industry. Now officially called Bengaluru. [place]

**Bangla** — The Bengali language. Preferred term over "Bengali" in contemporary usage, emphasizing the language's connection to Bengal rather than British colonial terminology. [language]

**Bangladeshi** — Of or relating to Bangladesh, the nation formed from East Bengal in 1971. [place]

**Bankim** — Bankim Chandra Chatterjee (1838-1894). Bengali novelist and journalist, author of "Anandamath" and composer of "Vande Mataram," a key figure in Bengali literary renaissance. [scholar]

**Baroda** — Former princely state in western India, now part of Gujarat. Known for progressive educational and social reforms under Maharaja Sayajirao Gaekwad III. [place]

**bhadralok** — The respectable people (Bengali). The Bengali middle class that emerged during colonial period, characterized by education, cultural refinement, and social status. [culture]

**bhajan** — Devotional song (Sanskrit/Hindi). Hindu religious music expressing love and devotion to deities. [term]

**Bharat** — Traditional Sanskrit name for India. Used in the Indian Constitution as "India, that is Bharat," representing indigenous cultural identity versus colonial "India." [culture]

**bharatiya** — Of or relating to Bharat/India (Sanskrit/Hindi). Adjective form emphasizing indigenous rather than colonial identity. [culture]

**Bhartrhari** — Sanskrit grammarian and philosopher (c. 450-510 CE). Author of "Vakyapadiya," influential work on language philosophy and grammar. [scholar]

**bhasha** — Language (Sanskrit/Hindi). Often used to distinguish vernacular languages from classical Sanskrit. [language]

**bhuutaani** — Beings, creatures (Sanskrit). Plural form used in the Upanishadic passage about beings arising from bliss. [term]

**Bihar** — State in eastern India, historically important center of Buddhist and Jain learning. [place]

**Braj** — Dialect of Hindi spoken in the Mathura-Vrindavan region of Uttar Pradesh, associated with Krishna devotional literature. [language]

**brhadaranyaka** — The "Great Forest" Upanishad (Sanskrit). One of the principal Upanishads, part of the Shatapatha Brahmana. [term]

## C

**Chandigarh** — Planned city serving as capital of Punjab and Haryana, designed by Le Corbusier. [place]

**Chatterjee** — Common Bengali surname. Refers to several prominent figures including novelist Bankim Chandra Chatterjee. [scholar]

**Chaudhuri** — Bengali surname. Notably Nirad C. Chaudhuri (1897-1999), controversial Bengali-English writer and cultural critic. [scholar]

**contextualization** — The process by which linguistic forms acquire specific meanings through their embedding in particular social and cultural contexts. [linguistics]

**coreferential** — In linguistics, referring to expressions that refer to the same entity in discourse. [linguistics]

## D

**Dacca** — Former name of Dhaka, now capital of Bangladesh. Important center of Bengali culture and education during colonial period. [place]

**dalits** — The oppressed (Sanskrit/Hindi). Preferred term for communities formerly called "untouchables" in the Indian caste system. [term]

**Dandin** — Sanskrit rhetorician and poet (c. 6th-7th century CE). Author of works on poetics and prose style. [scholar]

**darsana** — Philosophical viewpoint or school (Sanskrit). Literally "seeing" or "vision," referring to the six orthodox schools of Hindu philosophy. [term]

**Deccan** — The Deccan Plateau, peninsular India south of the Narmada River. Historically distinct cultural and political region. [place]

**desh** — Country, homeland (Sanskrit/Hindi/Bengali). Often used to evoke emotional connection to the land and cultural identity. [culture]

**dhaamaar** — Classical Hindustani musical form typically performed during the Holi festival. [term]

**Dhundhari** — Rajasthani dialect spoken in the Jaipur region. [language]

**dhrupad** — Ancient form of Hindustani classical music characterized by its meditative and spiritual nature. [term]

**diglossia/diglossic** — Sociolinguistic situation where two varieties of a language coexist, typically with one "high" (H) variety for formal contexts and one "low" (L) variety for informal use. [linguistics]

**Dogri** — Indo-Aryan language spoken in Jammu region and parts of Himachal Pradesh. [language]

**drashtavyah** — To be seen (Sanskrit gerundive). Philosophical term indicating what should or must be perceived or realized. [term]

**dvaapara** — The third of the four yugas or ages in Hindu cosmology. Precedes the current Kali Yuga. [term]

## E

**endoxa** — Generally accepted opinions or common beliefs (Greek, via Barthes). Used in cultural criticism to denote naturalized ideological assumptions. [linguistics]

## F

**FESH** — Formal Elaboration of Social Hierarchy. Linguistic feature characteristic of South and Southeast Asian languages involving systematic codification of social distinctions in grammar and vocabulary. [linguistics]

**feshified** — Having undergone FESH; marked by formal elaboration of social hierarchy in linguistic structure. [linguistics]

## G

**Gandhiji** — Mahatma Gandhi (1869-1948). Leader of Indian independence movement, advocate of nonviolent resistance and social reform. The suffix "-ji" indicates respect. [scholar]

**Ghose** — Bengali surname, notably of Aurobindo Ghose (Sri Aurobindo). [scholar]

**Gojri** — Language spoken by the Gujjar community in Jammu and Kashmir, Himachal Pradesh, and adjoining areas. [language]

**Gujarat** — State in western India, Gandhi's birthplace and important center of business and trade. [place]

**gurukul** — Traditional Hindu educational system where students lived with their teacher (guru) in an ashram-like setting. [culture]

## H

**Haryana** — State in northern India carved out from Punjab in 1966. [place]

**Himachal** — Himachal Pradesh, hill state in northern India with diverse Pahari dialects. [place]

**Hyderabad** — Historic princely state and city in south-central India, now capital of Telangana. Important center of Indo-Islamic culture. [place]

## I

**indo** — Prefix relating to India, used in linguistic terms like "Indo-Aryan" and "Indo-European." [linguistics]

**ishta** — Desired, wished (Sanskrit). Often used in compound "ishta-devata" (chosen deity) for one's personal divine focus. [term]

## J

**jaante** — (You/they) know (Bengali). Present tense form of the verb "to know." [term]

**jaataani** — Born ones (Sanskrit). Past participle referring to beings that have been born, from Upanishadic texts. [term]

**jaayante** — (They) are born (Sanskrit). Present passive form used in philosophical texts about the birth of beings. [term]

**Jammu** — Region in northern India, part of Jammu and Kashmir state. Dogri is the main language. [place]

**jana** — People, folk (Sanskrit). Root of terms like "janata" (populace) and "Jan Sangh" (political party). [term]

**Jaunsari** — Pahari language spoken in the Jaunsar-Bawar region of Uttarakhand. [language]

**Jharkhand** — State in eastern India created in 2000, carved out from Bihar. Rich in mineral resources. [place]

**jiivanti** — (They) live (Sanskrit). Present active form describing the life of beings in philosophical contexts. [term]

## K

**Kaalidaasa** — Classical Sanskrit poet and dramatist (c. 4th-5th century CE). Author of "Shakuntala" and other masterpieces, considered the greatest Sanskrit poet. [scholar]

**kaashii** — Ancient name for Varanasi (Sanskrit). Sacred city on the Ganges, center of Hindu learning and spirituality. [place]

**Kachru** — Braj Kachru (1932-2016). Influential Indian-American linguist who developed the "Three Circles" model of World Englishes. [scholar]

**Karnataka** — State in southern India, capital Bangalore. Formerly called Mysore. [place]

**Kerala** — State in southwestern India known for high literacy rates and progressive social indicators. [place]

**khalv** — Indeed, certainly (Sanskrit). Emphatic particle used in classical texts. [term]

**khayaal** — Imagination, thought (Urdu/Hindi). Also a form of classical Hindustani music emphasizing improvisation. [term]

**Kulvi** — Pahari dialect spoken in the Kullu valley of Himachal Pradesh. [language]

**Kumauni** — Pahari language spoken in the Kumaon region of Uttarakhand. [language]

## L

**Lakshadweep** — Union territory of India consisting of coral islands in the Arabian Sea. Malayalam and Mahl are the main languages. [place]

**lok** — People, folk (Sanskrit/Hindi). Used in compounds like "bhadralok" and political discourse about popular sovereignty. [term]

## M

**maarga** — Path, way (Sanskrit). Philosophical and spiritual term for a course of practice or conduct. [term]

**Madhya** — Central (Sanskrit/Hindi). Used in "Madhya Pradesh" (Central Province). [term]

**Mahabharata** — Great epic of ancient India (Sanskrit). One of the two major Sanskrit epics, containing the Bhagavad Gita. [term]

**Maharashtra** — State in western India, capital Mumbai. Marathi is the official language. [place]

**Mahasabha** — Great assembly (Sanskrit). Refers to the Hindu Mahasabha, a Hindu nationalist political organization. [term]

**maktab** — Elementary school (Arabic/Urdu). Traditional Islamic educational institution, often attached to mosques. [culture]

**Manipur** — State in northeast India with distinct culture and language (Manipuri/Meitei). [place]

**Manu** — Legendary lawgiver in Hindu tradition. The "Manusmriti" is an influential ancient legal text. [scholar]

**mantavyo** — To be thought/considered (Sanskrit gerundive). Philosophical term indicating what should be contemplated. [term]

**Marathis** — Speakers of Marathi language, primarily from Maharashtra state. [culture]

**metagrammatical** — Relating to grammar about grammar; discourse about grammatical rules and structures. [linguistics]

**Mewari** — Rajasthani dialect spoken in the former Mewar region of Rajasthan. [language]

**Mizoram** — State in northeast India, predominantly Christian with Mizo as the main language. [place]

## N

**Nadu** — Land, country (Tamil). Used in "Tamil Nadu" (land of the Tamils). [term]

**Nagaland** — State in northeast India inhabited by various Naga tribes with distinct languages and cultures. [place]

**navya-nyaaya** — New logic (Sanskrit). Medieval and early modern school of Indian philosophy that developed sophisticated logical methods. [term]

**Nehruvian** — Relating to Jawaharlal Nehru (1889-1964), India's first Prime Minister, or his political and economic philosophy emphasizing secularism, socialism, and modernization. [scholar]

**nididhyaasitavyo** — To be meditated upon (Sanskrit gerundive). Term from Vedantic texts indicating contemplative practice. [term]

**Nimadi** — Dialect spoken in the Nimar region of Madhya Pradesh. [language]

**nyaaya** — Logic, reasoning (Sanskrit). One of the six orthodox schools of Hindu philosophy. [term]

## O

**Orissa** — Former name of Odisha, state in eastern India known for Jagannath temple at Puri. [place]

## P

**Pāṇini** — Ancient Sanskrit grammarian (c. 4th century BCE). His "Ashtadhyayi" is a foundational text of linguistic analysis and Sanskrit grammar. [scholar]

**paracode** — Secondary or auxiliary code in multilingual contexts that supplements the primary linguistic system. [linguistics]

**paradharma** — Duty or law belonging to another (Sanskrit). In philosophical contexts, refers to following another's prescribed path rather than one's own. [term]

**pathsala** — Traditional school (Sanskrit). Elementary educational institution, often focusing on literacy and basic religious instruction. [culture]

**praakrta** — Natural, common (Sanskrit). Refers to Middle Indo-Aryan languages derived from Sanskrit. [language]

**praana** — Life breath, vital energy (Sanskrit). Fundamental concept in yoga and Ayurveda. [term]

**practitext** — Text embedded in and emerging from social practice rather than formal literary tradition. [linguistics]

**pradesh** — Province, state (Sanskrit). Used in names of Indian states like Uttar Pradesh, Madhya Pradesh. [term]

**Prakrits** — Middle Indo-Aryan languages that developed from Sanskrit, including Pali, Magadhi, and others used in early Buddhist and Jain literature. [language]

**Pune** — City in Maharashtra, formerly called Poona. Important center of Marathi culture and education. [place]

**Punjab** — State in northwestern India, divided at partition with western part in Pakistan. [place]

## R

**Rabindranath** — Rabindranath Tagore (1861-1941). Bengali polymath, Nobel Prize winner in Literature, composer of national anthems of India and Bangladesh. [scholar]

**Rajasthan** — State in northwestern India known for desert landscape, palaces, and rich cultural heritage. [place]

**Ramakrishna** — Sri Ramakrishna Paramahamsa (1836-1886). Bengali mystic and religious teacher, guru of Swami Vivekananda. [scholar]

**Ramanujan** — A.K. Ramanujan (1929-1993). Indian poet and scholar who wrote in English and Kannada, translator of classical Tamil and Sanskrit literature. [scholar]

**Ramayana** — Ancient Sanskrit epic about Prince Rama (Sanskrit). One of the two major Hindu epics. [term]

**Rammohun** — Ram Mohan Roy (1772-1833). Bengali social reformer, founder of Brahmo Samaj, advocate of women's rights and education. [scholar]

**reflexivizer** — Linguistic element that creates reflexive meaning, like "self" in English compound reflexives. [linguistics]

**renmozhi** — Beautiful language (Tamil). Term used to refer to Tamil's literary excellence. [term]

## S

**Sadani** — Language spoken in parts of Jharkhand, Bihar, and West Bengal. [language]

**sadhubhasha** — Refined/scholarly language (Bengali). The formal, Sanskritized register of Bengali used in literature and formal discourse. [language]

**sahitya** — Literature (Sanskrit). Comprehensive term for all forms of literary creation. [term]

**saṃskṛta** — Sanskrit, literally "refined, perfected" (Sanskrit). The classical language of Hindu texts and scholarship. [language]

**Saussure** — Ferdinand de Saussure (1857-1913). Swiss linguist whose structural approach to language influenced modern linguistics. [scholar]

**shaastras** — Sacred texts, scriptures (Sanskrit). Hindu religious and philosophical treatises covering various subjects. [term]

**shrutis** — That which is heard (Sanskrit). The Vedas and other revealed texts in Hindu tradition, distinguished from smrti (remembered texts). [term]

**smrti** — That which is remembered (Sanskrit). Hindu religious texts based on tradition and memory, distinguished from shruti. [term]

**srotavyo** — To be heard (Sanskrit gerundive). Referring to texts or teachings that should be listened to or studied. [term]

**swadeshi** — Of one's own country (Sanskrit). Movement promoting Indian-made goods and economic self-reliance during independence struggle. [culture]

## T

**Tagore** — Rabindranath Tagore (1861-1941). See Rabindranath above. Family name of the influential Bengali Tagore family. [scholar]

**taittiriiya** — Relating to the Taittiriya school (Sanskrit). Refers to the Taittiriya Upanishad, part of the Black Yajur Veda. [term]

**tatsama** — Same as that (Sanskrit). Words in modern Indian languages borrowed directly from Sanskrit without phonetic change. [term]

**thematization** — The process of making something an explicit topic or theme of discourse. [linguistics]

**thumrii** — Light classical Hindustani musical form characterized by romantic themes and flexible structure. [term]

**Tiruvananthapuram** — Capital of Kerala, formerly called Trivandrum. The name means "city of Ananta" (the cosmic serpent). [place]

**topicalization** — Linguistic process of moving an element to topic position for emphasis or focus. [linguistics]

**Travancore** — Former princely state in southern India, now part of Kerala. [place]

**tretaa** — The second of the four yugas (ages) in Hindu cosmology, following Satya Yuga. [term]

**Tripura** — Small state in northeast India, bordered by Bangladesh. [place]

## U

**upanishads** — Philosophical texts forming the conclusion of Vedic literature (Sanskrit). Foundation of Vedantic philosophy exploring the nature of reality and consciousness. [term]

**Uttar** — Northern (Sanskrit/Hindi). Used in "Uttar Pradesh" (Northern Province), India's most populous state. [term]

## V

**vaakvapadiya** — "On Words and Sentences" (Sanskrit). Bhartrhari's influential work on language philosophy and grammar. [term]

**vadamozhi** — Northern language (Tamil). Tamil term historically used to refer to Sanskrit. [term]

**vedas** — Sacred knowledge (Sanskrit). The oldest Hindu scriptures consisting of hymns, rituals, and philosophical speculation. [term]

**Vidyasagar** — Ishwar Chandra Vidyasagar (1820-1891). Bengali social reformer and educator, advocate for women's education and widow remarriage. [scholar]

**Vivekananda** — Swami Vivekananda (1863-1902). Bengali Hindu monk and philosopher, disciple of Ramakrishna, key figure in introducing Indian philosophy to the West. [scholar]

## Y

**yaajnavalkya** — Ancient Vedic sage (Sanskrit). Important figure in the Brihadaranyaka Upanishad, known for philosophical dialogues on the nature of self and reality. [scholar]

---

*This glossary serves as a guide to the specialized vocabulary that reflects the complex linguistic, cultural, and philosophical landscape of India as explored in Dasgupta's analysis of English as "India's Auntie Tongue."*
